Chapter 1

What Is Ayurveda?

The Science of Life

Ayurveda is more than an ancient Indian medical system. It is a way of living in harmony with nature. The word Ayurveda comes from two Sanskrit roots.

  • Ayus meaning life or vital energy
  • Veda meaning knowledge or science

Together it literally means “the science of life.”

Origin Of Ayurveda

Rooted in a tradition that dates back over 5,000 years, Ayurveda is one of humanity’s oldest systems of health and wellness in the world. According to traditional understanding, Ayurvedic knowledge was divinely revealed to sages (Rishis) during deep meditation. This wisdom was passed down orally from teacher to student and later recorded in classical texts.

Ayurveda is closely connected to the Vedas, especially the Atharva Veda, which contains early references to health, disease, and healing practices. Over time, Ayurveda was systematically organized into detailed medical knowledge by great scholars such as Charaka, Sushruta, and Vagbhata. Their works, including the Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita, and Ashtanga Hridaya, form the foundation of classical Ayurveda.

Unlike modern medicine, which often concentrates on managing symptoms, Ayurveda emphasizes harmony within the body and mind, focusing on preventing disease and addressing its underlying causes to restore lasting health.

Ayurveda – Definition

Charaka Samhita (Sutrasthana 1/41) gives the classical definition of Ayurveda.

Hitāhitaṁ sukhaṁ duḥkham āyu tasya hitāhitam
Mānaṁ ca tac ca yatroktam āyurvedaḥ sa ucyate

Ayurveda is the science that deals with the knowledge of life, explaining what is beneficial and harmful, and what leads to happiness and sorrow.

The Core Ideas of Ayurveda

Goal or Objective of Ayurveda

Swasthasya swasthya samrakṣaṇam
Āturasya vikāra praśamanam ca

The purpose of Ayurveda is to preserve the health of healthy individuals and to alleviate or cure diseases in the sick. Ayurveda strives to help you live a long, happy, and healthy life by aligning your body, mind, and spirit with the rhythms of nature.

Who is a Healthy Person in Ayurveda?

Sushruta Samhita describes the definition of a healthy person.

Samadoṣaḥ samāgniś ca samadhātu malakriyāḥ
Prasannātmendriyamanāḥ swastha ityabhidhīyate

A person is considered healthy when the doshas are in balance, the digestive fire (agni) functions properly, the body tissues (dhatus) are in equilibrium, the elimination of wastes (mala) occurs normally, and the mind, senses, and soul remain calm and content.

Health as Balance

In Ayurveda, health is balance. You are healthy when your body’s energies, digestion, tissues, wastes, and mind are all in harmony.

This balanced state is called Swastha, meaning “established in the Self.” When this balance is lost, illness appears. The Sanskrit term for disease, Vyadhi, literally means to be taken out of one’s natural state. This idea that imbalance creates illness lies at the heart of Ayurveda.

Jiva or Life as per Ayurveda

Śarīrendriya sattvātmā saṁyogo dhāri jīvitam

Life is sustained by the harmonious union of the body (śarīra), sense organs (indriya), mind (sattva), and soul (ātmā). Their coordinated functioning maintains existence and consciousness.

The Four Purusharthas

Dharma, Artha, Kāma, and Mokṣa are the four Purusharthas, or the four aims of human life in Indian philosophy.

  • Dharma – righteousness, duty, moral values, and ethical living
  • Artha – wealth, resources, and material prosperity
  • Kāma – desires, pleasures, and emotional fulfillment
  • Mokṣa – liberation and spiritual realization

By preserving health and treating disease, Ayurveda enables individuals to perform their duties, achieve prosperity, experience fulfillment, and progress toward spiritual liberation.

Modern Relevance of Ayurveda

In the 21st century, Ayurveda remains highly relevant as people increasingly seek natural, preventive, and holistic approaches to health. Modern lifestyles often lead to stress, poor digestion, sleep disturbances, and lifestyle-related disorders.

Ayurveda addresses these challenges through daily routines, balanced diet, mental well-being, and living in harmony with nature. Its principles encourage individuals to understand their body constitution and adopt mindful lifestyle choices.

Practices such as mindful eating, yoga, meditation, herbal preparations, and seasonal living support overall wellness and stress management in today's fast-paced world.

Today, Ayurveda continues to be practiced globally as a holistic system of well-being, emphasizing prevention, natural living, and balance between body, mind, and nature.
